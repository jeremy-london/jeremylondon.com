Small visual programs are not only toys. They are a way to make math and state visible. I have a soft spot for shader toys because they strip a problem down until it becomes obvious. A polished demo can hide a lot. A tiny shader cannot.

What I mean by that is simple. If the math is wrong, the gradient is wrong. If the timing is off, the motion stutters. If the buffers are not wired correctly, the whole thing shows it immediately. That kind of feedback is useful even when the final product is much more complicated, because it tells me whether the underlying shape is right before I waste time decorating it.

I also like shader toys because they let me move between curiosity and debugging without switching tools. Sometimes I am trying to understand how a visual effect works. Sometimes I am trying to catch a bug that only shows up when the scene updates in a certain order. A tiny shader can help with both. It is quick to tweak, quick to break, and quick to read once I stop pretending it is art first and code second.

That is probably why I keep writing about them. They are small enough to be honest. The polished version can come later.

If the toy reveals the bug, that is already a win. The final scene can wait until the math makes sense.
