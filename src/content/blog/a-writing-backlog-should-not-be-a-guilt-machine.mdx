I have ruined enough writing systems by making them too virtuous.

A writing backlog starts as a relief. Finally, a place to put the thought before it evaporates. A link, a sentence, a weird phrase from a meeting, the outline for a post I might write later. It feels responsible at first. Then the list gets heavy and the notes start sounding like obligations.

That is the failure mode I keep trying to avoid. A backlog should keep an idea alive long enough to matter. It should not keep a score.

## keep the list shy

The best backlog is the one that stays out of the way.

I do not want a dashboard. I do not want status fields. I do not want a tiny content factory pretending that a personal site is a newsroom. I want a place where a half-formed idea can sit still until I am ready to look at it again.

When the list starts asking for a weekly review, it is already doing too much. I am not building a procurement system. I am trying not to lose the thought that showed up while I was doing something else.

## three notes are usually enough

Most writing notes only need three things:

1. the sentence that made me stop
2. why it seemed worth keeping
3. the smallest next move

That last one matters more than people think. "Write about backlogs" is not enough. "Explain why old notes start to feel like chores" gives me an opening.

I do not want a backlog item to be complete. I want it to be restartable. A restartable note preserves the doorway, not the whole house.

## deleting is not a moral event

My biggest improvement here was deleting more of the backlog.

Not archiving. Deleting.

If a note has been sitting around for months and I feel nothing when I read it, it can go. If the title sounds like something a productivity app invented, it can go. If I only keep it because it once felt smart, it can go.

Keeping dead notes around is just rent collection on old curiosity.

## pull is the only metric that matters

I do not measure the backlog by size. I measure it by pull.

When I sit down to write, do any of the notes pull me back into the thought? Do they make me want to open the editor? Do they still feel attached to a real problem, not just to the version of me that wrote them down?

If the answer is yes, the backlog is working.

If the answer is no, the list is probably too polite. "Thoughts on developer experience" is empty. "Good CLI output is a kind of API" has a pulse.

Guilt is a bad fuel for writing. It can make me open the backlog, but it cannot make the draft better. Curiosity, irritation, and the sense that I finally know how to explain something are much better signals.

## leave room to throw things away

The backlog should make writing available, not morally urgent.

That means the default stays plain. Capture the idea. Add one sentence of context. Add the next move if I know it. Revisit it when I am actually looking for something to write. Delete it when it goes cold.

The list is allowed to remember. It is not allowed to accuse.
