I would keep math small here. The useful part is Stable notation and repeated small examples do more for intuition than a tour through every formula. That is enough material for a math note, not a manifesto.

The math mistake is treating the observation as if it needs a complete theory around it. In practice, technical learning work usually gets better when the first version names the boundary and then stops. For math, that boundary is where explanation stops and experimentation starts.

The math check I would use is simple: can the next person see what changed and why it matters? If the answer is yes, the math post has done its job. If the answer is no, adding three more math paragraphs will not fix it.

The thing I would not add to math is a fake architecture section. A short math note can still be technical if it names the pressure clearly: who is acting, what state is being touched, and what evidence would make the claim falsifiable. That is enough for math. The rest of math can wait until there is a real example to inspect.

I am leaving math as a short note because the idea is useful at this size. A longer math version would need a real system diagram, a failure example, and evidence. Without those, more length would just be decoration.
