Personal automation is easier to trust when it leaves a plain record of what it did and why. The point is not to make automation sound bigger than it is. It is to name the decision it changes.

## The Practical Part

Automation gets interesting when it changes a real workflow. The example I would use is Personal automation is easier to trust when it leaves a plain record of what it did and why, because it gives the reader something to inspect instead of a broad claim to accept.

## The Edge Case

The failure mode for automation is the system depends on future energy it has no right to assume. For automation, that can look fine from a distance, so the page ships, the demo runs, or the team agrees with itself while the next person still cannot tell what actually happened.

## What I Want To See

The check for automation is the small habit that still works when the week is crowded. For automation, that is enough to separate a useful note from a long one.

I do not want automation to sound more certain than the evidence allows. Make the decision visible, then stop.
