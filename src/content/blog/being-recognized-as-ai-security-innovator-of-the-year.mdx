I was recognized as AI Security Innovator of the Year in the 2026 Cybersecurity Stars Awards from The Hacker News. The official page lists me as Director of Engineering, AI & Threat Analytics at Keeper Security, but the part I care about is more specific: the award points at a body of work around AI-native threat detection, AI agent governance, and keeping credentials out of developer chat workflows.

That is the work I have been trying to make real.

The award page calls out three things: KeeperAI, Keeper's Model Context Protocol integration, and the Keeper Agent Kit. Those are different products, but they all come from the same pressure I have felt for a while now. AI does not get to be a special exception to security architecture. If an AI agent is touching privileged systems, secrets, session data, or enterprise workflows, it needs the same zero-trust discipline we expect from humans and machines.

KeeperAI is the most direct version of that idea. Privileged session monitoring has a bad coverage problem. Analysts cannot manually review everything, and the sessions they miss are often the ones that matter later. The work behind KeeperAI was about moving threat detection closer to the live session, where commands and behavior can be evaluated as they happen instead of after the damage is already done.

The numbers on the awards page are strong: full privileged-session coverage, a major reduction in detection time, fewer false positives, and a large productivity gain for analysts. I am proud of that, but the number I care about most is the coverage. If a system only watches a small slice of privileged activity, the rest of the environment is running on hope.

The MCP integration came from a newer problem. AI agents are becoming normal inside enterprise workflows, but access control around them is still too loose in a lot of systems. An agent should not inherit vague authority because it sounds helpful. It should get scoped access, human confirmation for sensitive operations, and an audit trail that security teams can actually use.

The Keeper Agent Kit attacks a related leak: secrets ending up in chat. Developers are moving fast with coding agents, and the easiest thing in the moment is often the riskiest thing long term. Paste the API key. Paste the database credential. Paste the thing that should never have entered a third-party prompt window. The Agent Kit is meant to remove that temptation by resolving secrets at runtime through hardened Keeper tooling instead of exposing raw credentials in the conversation.

That is the through line for me: AI should be useful without becoming exempt from the rules that made the system safe in the first place.

I do not want AI security to become a pile of slogans. "Governance" can get vague fast. The test is whether the architecture changes the default behavior. Does the agent get less access by default? Does a human approve the dangerous move? Does the log explain what happened? Does the credential stay out of the chat transcript? Does the analyst get a concrete signal instead of another noisy alert?

Those are the questions I want the work to answer.

Recognition is nice. I am not going to pretend otherwise. But the better feeling is seeing this category get taken seriously. AI agents are becoming part of the enterprise attack surface, and security teams need tools that understand that without making every workflow miserable.

The award page is here: [AI Security Innovator of the Year](https://awards.thehackernews.com/winners/2026/keeper-ai-threat-detection/).

I am grateful for the recognition, and I am more convinced than ever that the next phase of AI security belongs to boring, enforceable controls: least privilege, audited actions, safe secret resolution, and threat detection that works while the session is still alive.
