Flags are not just rollout switches. They keep a record of the argument behind a release. I kept coming back to that while I was writing this because the thing that hurts later is rarely the launch itself. It is the cleanup.

I have seen teams treat flags as temporary scaffolding and then leave them around so long that nobody remembers the original risk. That is when a flag stops being useful. It still changes behavior, but it no longer tells you anything. A good flag should answer a few plain questions without forcing anyone to open a ticket archaeology project. Why did we add it? Who owns it? What breaks if it stays on too long?

That part matters more than the rollout ceremony. I care about the boring afterwork, when someone on the team has to decide whether to keep the old path alive for another week because one customer is still on it, or whether it is finally safe to remove the branch entirely. That is where flags start acting like memory. They remember which ideas were tested, which ones were nervous guesses, and which ones never deserved to stay.

The worst version is a flag with a vague name and no owner. I do not mean a philosophical owner. I mean the person who knows whether the thing is still doing work or just occupying space. If the name only says `newCheckout`, that tells me almost nothing. If the name is `checkoutAddressAutocomplete` or `checkoutTaxFallback`, I can at least guess what kind of cleanup is waiting for me.

I also think flags are a useful check on how honest a product team is being with itself. If the team keeps adding flags and never removes them, that usually means the release process is too nervous or the cleanup step is too weak. If the team removes them too quickly, they are probably pretending rollout risk does not exist. The balance is the useful part.

When I write about product work now, I try to keep that in mind. A release is not finished when the code ships. It is finished when the shortcut is gone, the flag is deleted, and the team no longer needs to remember why the detour existed in the first place.
