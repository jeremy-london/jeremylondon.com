Offline capability is not a storage trick. It is a promise about what the user can trust when the network disappears. I keep coming back to that because "offline-first" gets used loosely, and it should not. If the app can edit locally but leaves the user guessing about whether the data actually made it home, then the promise is weak.

The part I care about most is recovery. Working offline is the easy part to explain. The hard part is what happens when the device comes back, the sync queue starts moving, and two edits collide. That is where the product either earns trust or loses it. I do not want an offline feature that only works when everything is calm. That is not a feature. That is a demo.

I have a lot of respect for the small details here. Sync indicators, local persistence, retry handling, merge rules, and the way conflicts are shown to the user all matter more than the slogan. If those pieces are vague, people get nervous and start not trusting the app even when the data is safe. If those pieces are clear, the app feels sturdy in a way that is hard to fake.

I also think offline-first forces teams to be more honest about what the app is for. If every action needs the network, then you have not really designed a local workflow. You have just hidden latency. Once you start building for disconnected use, the product spec gets sharper fast because you have to choose what survives on the device and what waits for the server.

That is why I treat offline-first as a promise, not a storage technique. It is a commitment to behavior, and behavior is what users remember when the network drops.
