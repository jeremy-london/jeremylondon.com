Maintenance days count.

Maintenance days are not a pause from the work. They are the work that keeps the next few weeks from going sideways.

## what gets fixed

The useful work is often small and unglamorous: delete the dead script, fix the stale README, clean the notes, repair the smoke test, remove the flag that no longer matters.

I like a maintenance day when I can point at the outcome without inventing a bigger story. The repo is easier to read. The app starts a little faster. The note backlog has fewer landmines. The build log has one less mystery.

That is enough.

## what I do not want

I do not want maintenance to become a ceremony around maintenance. I do not need a planning document for every broken shortcut. I do not need a productivity narrative around deleting a stale file.

The work is boring in the useful sense. It clears out drift. It removes the old assumptions that quietly made everything harder.

## why it still matters

Maintenance pays off when the next person can tell what still works and what needs attention.

I like maintenance when it makes the system more legible. If the next person can tell what still works and what needs attention, the day paid for itself.

The trick is to stop treating that as second-class work. It is not second-class. It is the part that keeps the rest of the system honest.
