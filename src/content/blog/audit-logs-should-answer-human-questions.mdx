Logs should tell a person what happened, who did it, and what changed without a forensic treasure hunt.

I would keep audit logs small here. The useful part is Logs should tell a person what happened, who did it, and what changed without a forensic treasure hunt. That is enough material for a audit logs note, not a manifesto.

The audit logs mistake is treating the observation as if it needs a complete theory around it. In practice, security systems work usually gets better when the first version names the boundary and then stops. For audit logs, that boundary is who is allowed to act on which resource, with which evidence.

The audit logs check I would use is simple: can the next person see what changed and why it matters? If the answer is yes, the audit logs post has done its job. If the answer is no, adding three more audit logs paragraphs will not fix it.

The thing I would not add to audit logs is a fake architecture section. A short audit logs note can still be technical if it names the pressure clearly: who is acting, what state is being touched, and what evidence would make the claim falsifiable. That is enough for audit logs. The rest of audit logs can wait until there is a real example to inspect.

I am leaving audit logs as a short note because the idea is useful at this size. A longer audit logs version would need a real system diagram, a failure example, and evidence. Without those, more length would just be decoration.
