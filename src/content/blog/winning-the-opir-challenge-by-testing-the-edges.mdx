I won one of the first-place spots in the Opir Challenge, and the part that stuck with me most was not the score. It was how much the interview ended up sounding like the way I actually think about model work.

Aleks Smechov kicked things off by saying my approach was the most unusual one he had seen in the challenge. That tracks. I have never been the person who starts with a giant model and hopes it absorbs the problem by force. I started as a front-end engineer, moved into data and machine learning because the teams around me needed software around models, then spent years in defense and applied ML at Lockheed Martin before moving into startups and, eventually, Keeper Security. That path taught me the same lesson over and over: the best results usually come from understanding the constraint, not pretending it is not there.

At Lockheed, the constraint was size and deployment. We were trying to run models on helicopters and other tight environments where compute was not free and latency mattered. Sometimes that meant a 4 GB GPU and a lot of ugly compromises. Later, at Keeper, the constraints changed but the logic did not. We work under zero-knowledge and zero-trust assumptions, so the models we build have to be small, CPU-bound, and useful without assuming access to the user’s data. That pushes me toward lightweight encoders, classifiers, and small foundation models that can actually run where the product lives.

That background is why Opir made sense to me immediately. I saw a multitask safety model and thought, okay, this is a classifier problem with a lot of surface area. The first thing I wanted to understand was the tokenizer. If the tokenizer does not represent the input well, the rest of the stack starts from bad footing. That is especially true in multilingual systems. If the language or script is outside the model’s comfort zone, the classifier can end up staring at a pile of unknown tokens instead of a real signal.

So that is where I started. I leaned into multilingual input the model had not really been trained on, then pushed further into obscure or low-corpus languages and formats. I tried to get the classifier to admit it did not know what it was looking at. Once that happened, the next question was whether I could keep the underlying LLM from making the right inference anyway.

That became the real shape of the attack. I was not looking for one dramatic jailbreak string. I was trying to split the problem in two. First, get past the safety classifier by making the request hard to recognize. Then get the model itself to follow the wrong path by hiding the useful part of the instruction in a structure the classifier did not handle well. I used multilingual obfuscation, then layered in an obscure programming-language framing, and then encoded part of the request so the system was forced to reason about it indirectly. The point was to get the model to process the request as something else long enough for the safety check to miss the actual intent.

The part that mattered most was that the attack was not just linguistic. It was architectural. I was probing the difference between what the classifier thought the request was and what the base model actually did with it. When the request was split across different forms, the classifier focused on one part and the LLM filled in the other. That is how the break happened. Not with one magic phrase. With enough shape-shifting that the front end of the system and the back end stopped agreeing with each other.

Once I had a working path, I kept iterating on it with other obscure languages and variations just to see whether the weakness was specific or general. It was useful for me personally because I was also comparing it against our own prompt-injection work at Keeper. We have the same class of problem there, just under harsher deployment constraints. If a model collapses into unknown tokens for a large slice of the data, then a classifier that treats that as ordinary input is going to miss things it should not miss.

The broader conversation in the interview was about where AI security goes from here. I think the answer is that it gets more automated on both sides. Offense gets faster because agents can probe more combinations than a human can. Defense has to move just as fast, which is why I care so much about small models that can run locally, on the CPU, without a huge latency penalty. You do not want your safety layer to be so heavy that it is useless in the moment you need it.

We also talked about the other end of the problem: social engineering, bot swarms, cloned voices, and the way attacks stop looking like single exploits and start looking like campaigns. That is where I think the next real pressure lands. A lot of the old assumptions around trust were built for a world where the attack surface moved slower than the defender. That is changing.

I came away from the challenge with a lot of respect for the model design. Opir was not easy to fool, and that was the point. It forced me to think about tokenization, unknowns, multilingual coverage, and the gap between classifier logic and model behavior. That is exactly the kind of pressure test I want on anything that claims to make a security decision.

The interview version of the win is basically this: I got there by treating the model like a system with seams, not like a single brain. I looked for where the seams did not line up, and I kept pulling until one of them gave.
