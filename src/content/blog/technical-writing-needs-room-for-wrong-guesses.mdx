## the assumption underneath teaching mistakes

The diagram for teaching mistakes probably hides the work around the edges. Diagrams are good at boxes and arrows. They are bad at time, ownership, retries, permissions, naming, and the odd little checks that keep a workflow from surprising people.

## a better failure case

Teaching mistakes fails quietly when the failure still produces something that looks acceptable. The answer returns. The page renders. The script exits zero. The problem is that the output is now missing context, using the wrong assumption, or teaching the reader the wrong lesson.

When I read a post about teaching mistakes, I want at least one paragraph about the quiet failure. If the article cannot name the boring way the idea breaks, it probably has not earned the confident parts yet.

## the part the team owns

The example I trust for teaching mistakes is small enough to run and specific enough to be wrong. That second part matters. If the example cannot fail in an instructive way, it is not an example. It is decoration with syntax highlighting.

That is why a learner getting the right output for the wrong reason works better than a generic setup. It gives the example a little dirt under its fingernails. The reader can imagine the next step instead of only admiring the shape of the explanation.

## what the logs should say

Instrumentation around teaching mistakes should answer human questions. What happened? What input did we use? Who had access? Which version ran? What changed since the last successful run? If the logs cannot answer those questions, the team will invent a story under pressure.

## the test that earns trust

Review changes teaching mistakes because it forces the idea to leave the author's head. The author knows which corner was cut. The reviewer only sees the artifact. That mismatch is irritating, but it is useful.

For teaching mistakes, I would ask the reviewer to point at one sentence that feels too broad and one sentence that feels genuinely useful. Then I would edit toward the useful one.

## when the simple answer is enough

The maintenance version of teaching mistakes is less exciting than the first version. It asks who updates the example, who owns the dependency, what breaks when the surrounding code moves, and whether the post still tells the truth six months later.

## what I would cut

The interface around teaching mistakes should reduce guessing. If the reader or user needs to know state, show state. If they need to understand a boundary, name the boundary. If the next step is risky, make the risk concrete before asking for approval.

## the handoff note

The note I would leave about teaching mistakes would be short and practical: here is the constraint, here is the failure I am worried about, and here is the check that gives me confidence. That is enough to help the next person without pretending to settle the topic forever.

For this topic, the position is that teaching mistakes needs evidence around it. The evidence might be a runnable example, a browser check, a log line, a permission test, or a category count. Without evidence, the post is only a polished claim.

## after the cleanup

The second pass on teaching mistakes is where I would cut the generic parts. If a paragraph could appear in ten other posts, it has not earned the space yet. The rewrite should move closer to the actual scene, the real constraint, or the uncomfortable tradeoff.

## the teardown

The reason teaching mistakes keeps showing up is not that the idea is hard to name. It is that the idea sits in the gap between the clean version people describe and the version they have to operate. That gap is where most of the useful writing lives.

I would start with a learner getting the right output for the wrong reason. It gives the reader something to hold. A concrete scene also makes the tradeoff less slippery because the reader can ask what would happen next, who would notice, and what evidence would be left behind.

## what broke first

The first rough version of teaching mistakes usually looks better than it behaves. It has the right nouns, a reasonable diagram, and a few confident sentences. Then one ordinary detail changes and the explanation has to admit what it left out.

## what I would keep about teaching mistakes

The part I would keep is the constraint. Teaching mistakes gets easier to reason about when the team can see the boundary, run the example, and inspect the evidence. That is not a grand conclusion. It is just the difference between a note that feels useful once and a system that keeps helping after the first read.
