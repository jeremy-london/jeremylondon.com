An eval dashboard turns model examples into a clearer view of quality, regressions, latency, and cost. I would keep the argument close to the workflow instead of treating evals like a slogan.

## Why It Matters

For evals, the important part is a team arguing about whether the model improved or merely changed. That gives the idea a place to stand. Without that concrete surface, the post turns into category talk: constraints, examples, and maintenance.

## The Risk

The bad version of evals is familiar: the explanation sounds plausible while the actual decision stays vague. For evals, sometimes that means a decision with no trail, a boundary with no owner, or an example that does not prove the point.

## My Bar

For evals, I would ask for the example or constraint that makes the point inspectable. The evals post can be brief and still be useful if that evidence is present.

The smaller version is stronger. It gives the reader the evals claim, the failure mode, and the check without pretending there is a larger theory hiding underneath.
