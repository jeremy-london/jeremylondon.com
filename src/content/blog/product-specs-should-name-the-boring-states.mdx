A product spec that skips loading, empty, error, permission, and rollback states is not finished. I would keep the argument close to the workflow instead of treating product specs like a slogan.

## Why It Matters

For product specs, the important part is a product spec that skips loading, empty, error, permission, and rollback states is not finished. That gives the idea a place to stand. Without that concrete surface, the post turns into category talk: state, handoff, forms, product quality, and boring edge cases.

## The Risk

The bad version of product specs is familiar: the feature works in the happy path but leaves the user guessing at the boundary. For product specs, sometimes that means a decision with no trail, a boundary with no owner, or an example that does not prove the point.

## My Bar

For product specs, I would ask for the state the interface needs to make visible. The product specs post can be brief and still be useful if that evidence is present.

The smaller version is stronger. It gives the reader the product specs claim, the failure mode, and the check without pretending there is a larger theory hiding underneath.
