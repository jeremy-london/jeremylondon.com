A product spec that skips loading, empty, error, permission, and rollback states is not finished.

I would keep product specs small here. The useful part is a product spec that skips loading, empty, error, permission, and rollback states is not finished. That is enough material for a product specs note, not a manifesto.

The product specs mistake is treating the observation as if it needs a complete theory around it. In practice, product engineering work usually gets better when the first version names the boundary and then stops. For product specs, that boundary is what the interface must make visible before the user can act.

The product specs check I would use is simple: can the next person see what changed and why it matters? If the answer is yes, the product specs post has done its job. If the answer is no, adding three more product specs paragraphs will not fix it.

The thing I would not add to product specs is a fake architecture section. A short product specs note can still be technical if it names the pressure clearly: who is acting, what state is being touched, and what evidence would make the claim falsifiable. That is enough for product specs. The rest of product specs can wait until there is a real example to inspect.

I am leaving product specs as a short note because the idea is useful at this size. A longer product specs version would need a real system diagram, a failure example, and evidence. Without those, more length would just be decoration.
