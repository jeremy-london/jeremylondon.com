Gemma and Phi-3 were easy to underestimate if you only watched frontier leaderboards. Google released Gemma 2B and 7B in February 2024. Microsoft followed with Phi-3 in April, including a 3.8B mini model aimed at the small language model category.

I liked those releases because they made "small" sound less like a compromise. A small model that can run locally, fine-tune cheaply, or sit close to private data changes the product shape. It does not need to be the smartest model in the building. It needs to be good enough at a narrow job and cheap enough to run often.

Gemma's connection to Gemini mattered. Google was taking technology and tooling from the Gemini era and giving developers open model weights in sizes that could run on more ordinary hardware. That made the release feel like a bridge between large hosted intelligence and practical experimentation.

Phi-3 made a different point. Microsoft pushed hard on data quality and showed that a 3.8B model could compete with much larger models on selected language, coding, and math evaluations. I would not overread the benchmarks, but the direction was right. Better data and careful training can make small models useful in places where a large model is too slow, too expensive, or too hard to ship.

The product lesson was not "replace the frontier model." The product lesson was to split the stack. Use a small model for fast classification, rewriting, routing, extraction, local hints, and privacy-sensitive support. Escalate when the task deserves deeper reasoning.

The annoying part is that small models make evaluation more important, not less. They will fail differently. They may be excellent on the narrow path and brittle just outside it. That is fine if the product admits the boundary.

By spring 2024, small models were no longer only a hobbyist topic. They were starting to look like infrastructure.
